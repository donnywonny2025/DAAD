# Jeff Kerr — Case Narrative & Legal Context

## Jeff's Story

- **Age at time of incidents:** ~18 years old (born ~1977-1978)
- **Year:** ~1999

- **Incident 2:** Second OUIL approximately 1 year later
- **Result:** Automatic, indefinite license revocation under Michigan law

---

## Critical Context: The Law Was Different in 1999

### What Jeff faced (1999 rules):
- The charge was **OUIL** (Operating Under the Influence of Liquor) — a term that no longer exists
- The legal BAC limit was **.10%** — Jeff blew below it on the first charge but could still be convicted based on officer observation
- Two OUILs within 7 years = **automatic indefinite revocation**
- **No restricted license option** for second offenders
- **No Sobriety Court program** — didn't exist until January 1, 2011 (MCL 257.304)
- **No ignition interlock (BAIID) pathway** — while BAIIDs existed in Michigan law since 1988, they were not integrated into restricted licensing for second offenders until much later
- The only path back was a formal SOS administrative hearing after the mandatory waiting period

### What would happen TODAY (2026 rules):
- The charge would be **OWI** (Operating While Intoxicated) — terminology changed September 30, 2003
- The legal BAC limit is **.08%** (lowered from .10% under federal pressure)
- Below .08% would likely be charged as **OWVI** (Operating While Visibly Impaired) — a lesser offense
- **Sobriety Court** would be available — allows restricted license with BAIID after 45-day hard suspension
- **Ignition interlock** pathway exists for early restricted privileges
- Modern treatment and monitoring options (GPS ankle monitors, continuous alcohol monitoring) provide alternatives to full revocation
- Greater emphasis on rehabilitation over pure punishment

### The Argument This Creates:
An 18-year-old kid in 1999 got the same permanent revocation that a hardened repeat offender would get — because the system offered **zero middle ground**. Today, that same situation would likely result in Sobriety Court, a restricted license, an interlock device, and a clear path back to full driving privileges. Jeff didn't get any of those options. He got a life sentence for a problem that modern law treats very differently.

That's 25+ years without a license for something that today would have a structured resolution path.

## The Florida License Situation

### What Happened:
- After the Michigan revocation, Jeff moved to Florida
- Obtained a Florida driver's license — the PDPS interstate check either wasn't queried or had a data gap (common in early 2000s)
- Renewed the FL license at least once (FL licenses last 7-10 years)
- Drove legally in Florida for years on a valid FL license

### How the Hearing Officer Will View This:
The research is clear — the hearing officer's **primary focus is sobriety and rehabilitation**, not the FL license. However:

- It **could** be flagged as a credibility issue — "you knew Michigan revoked you and obtained a license elsewhere"
- It will NOT be treated as a criminal charge at the hearing — this is an administrative proceeding
- Many petitioners have this exact same situation — it's extremely common for people revoked in MI to get licenses in other states, especially pre-2010 when interstate data sharing was weak

### Strategic Framing for Testimony:
If asked about the Florida license, the answer is straightforward and honest:
- "I moved to Florida and applied for a license. Florida issued one. I drove responsibly on it for years."
- Do NOT volunteer extra detail or apologize excessively — that signals guilt about something that isn't the point of this hearing
- Redirect to what matters: "My driving record in Florida was clean. I drove responsibly. My focus today is demonstrating that my substance use is under control and will remain under control."

### Why This Actually Helps:
- Years of driving in FL with a **clean record** = proof Jeff is a responsible driver
- No additional DUI/OWI/OUIL incidents in FL or AZ = evidence the substance issue was a brief chapter at age 18, not a pattern
- The clean FL driving history SUPPORTS the "under control and likely to remain under control" argument
